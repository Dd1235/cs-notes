Expectation, Linearity & Indicators

2 min read · source: quant/expectation.md

Linearity of expectation is the single highest-value trick in interview probability: it works even when events are dependent.

The rule

E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y]. Always. No independence needed. That's the whole superpower.

Analogy: expected total weight of a truckload = sum of expected weights of the boxes — even if heavy boxes tend to travel together.

The indicator trick

To count "how many of these things happen", define Xᵢ = 1 if thing i happens, else 0. Then:

E[count] = Σ E[Xᵢ] = Σ P(thing i happens)

Turn a nasty counting problem into a sum of easy probabilities.

Three classics, solved in one line each

Hat check. n people grab random hats. Expected number with their own hat?
E = Σᵢ P(person i gets own hat) = n · (1/n) = 1. (Independent of n — great "wow" answer.)

Fixed points, pairs, anything. Expected number of adjacent HH pairs in n coin flips: (n−1) · 1/4. Just indicators over positions.

Coupon collector. n coupon types, draw uniformly; expected draws to collect all?
Waiting for the k-th new coupon is geometric with p = (n−k+1)/n, so
E = n·(1/1 + 1/2 + … + 1/n) = n·Hₙ ≈ n ln n.
For n = 50: ≈ 225 draws.

When linearity isn't enough

  • Variance does not add for dependent variables: Var(X+Y) = Var X + Var Y + 2Cov(X,Y).
  • E[XY] = E[X]·E[Y] needs independence (or at least zero covariance).
  • E[g(X)] ≠ g(E[X]) in general — Jensen's inequality gives the direction (convex g: ≥).

Drill

  1. 100 passengers, first one sits randomly, everyone else takes their own seat if free else random. P(last passenger gets own seat)? (1/2 — symmetry between seat #1 and seat #100.)
  2. Expected number of distinct birthdays in a room of 30? (365·(1 − (364/365)³⁰))
  3. n couples at a party, random circular seating — expected number of couples sitting together? Set up the indicators.

Seen at: D. E. Shaw, Citadel; airplane-seat is a phone-screen staple everywhere.
