Birthday, Monty Hall & Friends

2 min read · source: quant/paradoxes.md

Three problems that sound trivial and aren't. Each has one sentence that dissolves the confusion — memorize the sentence, re-derive the rest.

Birthday paradox

23 people → >50% chance two share a birthday. 57 people → 99%.

Work with the complement: P(all distinct) = 365/365 · 364/365 · … and use 1−x ≈ e^(−x):
P(all n distinct) ≈ exp(−n(n−1)/730). Set to ½ → n ≈ 23.

The dissolving sentence: you're not comparing 23 people to you — you're comparing C(23,2) = 253 pairs, and 253 lottery tickets at 1/365 each is a lot.

Interview variant: how many people until someone shares your birthday? ~253 (now it is per-person, 1−(364/365)ⁿ = ½).

Monty Hall

3 doors, 1 car. You pick one; the host — who knows where the car is — opens a different door showing a goat, and offers a switch. Switch: 2/3 win.

Your first pick Host opens Switching gets you
car (p = 1/3) either goat goat ✗
goat A (p = 1/3) goat B (forced) car ✓
goat B (p = 1/3) goat A (forced) car ✓

The dissolving sentence: the host's reveal is not random — he filters his side of the split, so your initial 1/3 never gets updated, and the remaining 2/3 collapses onto one door.

If the host opens a random door and it happens to show a goat, switching is 50/50 — the information content of the reveal is what matters. Say this unprompted and the interviewer is done.

Boy–girl phrasing traps

Two children, each B/G equally likely:

  • "The elder is a boy" → P(both boys) = 1/2 (only GG eliminated... specifically {BB, BG} remain from ordering elder-younger).
  • "At least one is a boy" → P(both boys) = 1/3 (BB out of {BB, BG, GB}).
  • "At least one is a boy born on a Tuesday" → 13/27 (!). The more specific the identifying detail, the closer to 1/2 — specificity makes it more like pointing at a particular child.

The dissolving sentence: the answer depends on the procedure that generated the statement, not the statement alone.

Drill

  1. In a room of 30, expected number of shared-birthday pairs? (C(30,2)/365 ≈ 1.19 — indicators again.)
  2. Monty Hall with 100 doors, host opens 98 goats — switch odds? (99/100; the exaggeration is the best intuition pump.)
  3. A family has two children and you meet one at random: a boy. P(both boys)? (1/2 — you sampled a child, not a statement.)
