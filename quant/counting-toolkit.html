Counting — Stars, Bars & Derangements

2 min read · source: quant/combinatorics.md

Most counting questions are one of five shapes. Identify the shape, apply the formula, sanity-check with a tiny case.

The five shapes

Shape Formula Recognize it by
Ordered pick k of n n!/(n−k)! "arrange", "sequence", "podium"
Unordered pick C(n,k) "choose a committee"
Multiset / identical balls into boxes C(n+k−1, k−1) stars & bars — "distribute n identical"
Arrangements with repeats n!/(a!b!…) "letters of MISSISSIPPI"
At-least/at-most constraints inclusion–exclusion "none of…", "at least one of…"

Stars & bars in one picture

Distribute 7 identical coins to 3 people: ★★|★★★★|★ — arrange 7 stars and 2 bars → C(9,2) = 36. Requiring everyone ≥1: give each a coin first, then distribute 4 → C(6,2) = 15.

Inclusion–exclusion & derangements

|A ∪ B ∪ C| = Σ|single| − Σ|pairs| + |triple| — add, over-counted, subtract, over-subtracted, add back.

Derangements (no one gets their own hat): Dₙ = n!·(1 − 1/1! + 1/2! − … ± 1/n!) → Dₙ ≈ n!/e.
So P(no fixed point) → 1/e ≈ 37%. Pair this with the expectation result (expected fixed points = 1, see expectation) for a complete interview answer.

1/e shows up everywhere: derangements, the secretary problem (reject the first 37%, then take the next best — wins with probability 1/e), P(a particular coupon missed in n draws of n).

Ballot problem & Catalan

Candidate A gets a votes, B gets b (a > b). P(A leads throughout the count) = (a−b)/(a+b) — proved with the reflection principle.

Catalan numbers Cₙ = C(2n,n)/(n+1) count: balanced bracket sequences, monotone lattice paths that never cross the diagonal, binary tree shapes. If a problem says "never goes negative", think Catalan/reflection.

Drill

  1. How many non-negative integer solutions to x+y+z = 10 with x ≤ 4? (All: C(12,2)=66; subtract x ≥ 5 via substitution: C(7,2)=21 → 45.)
  2. 8 people, round table, two specific people must not be adjacent — arrangements? (Circular total 7! minus glued 2·6!.)
  3. Random permutation of 52 cards — P(no card is in its original position)? (≈ 1/e.)

Seen at: D. E. Shaw OA aptitude sections lean heavily on shapes 3 and 5.
