Markov Chains, Minimally

2 min read · source: quant/markov.md

You don't need the full theory — you need states, transition arrows, and two question templates: "long-run fraction" and "expected steps to absorption".

The model

A system hops between finitely many states; the next state depends only on the current one (memorylessness — the chain has no diary, only a location).

Write it as a transition matrix P where P[i][j] = P(next = j | now = i). Rows sum to 1.

Analogy: a board game where your next square depends only on where you stand and a dice roll — not on how you got there.

Template 1 — long-run fraction (stationary distribution)

π = πP: the distribution that the chain settles into. For small chains, solve the linear system + "π sums to 1".

Quick example — weather: sunny→sunny 0.9, rainy→sunny 0.5. Solve π_s = 0.9π_s + 0.5π_r with π_s+π_r = 1 → sunny 5/6 of days. Two equations, done.

Template 2 — expected steps to absorption

Some states are absorbing (once in, never out — ruin, jackpot, "game over"). For each transient state i:

Eᵢ = 1 + Σⱼ P[i][j]·Eⱼ, with E = 0 at absorbing states.

This is the first-step analysis you already used for waiting times and gambler's ruin — Markov chains are just the general framing.

Interview-ready facts

  • Memorylessness is the definition; if a problem's future depends only on "where you are now", model it as a chain.
  • P(state after n steps) = row of Pⁿ — matrix power = n-step transition.
  • A chain with all states reachable and no forced cycles (irreducible, aperiodic) has a unique stationary distribution, reached from any start.
  • PageRank is literally a stationary distribution: a random surfer's long-run page-visit fractions.

Drill

  1. Mouse in a 3-room house with doors in a line (1–2–3), moves to a random adjacent room each minute, cat waits in room 3. Starting in room 1, expected minutes to get caught? (Set E₁, E₂; answer 4.)
  2. A die is rolled repeatedly; you track "sum mod 3". Write the 3×3 transition matrix — what's the stationary distribution and why is it obvious? (Uniform, by symmetry.)
  3. For the weather chain above, P(rain two days from a sunny day)? (Compute the (s,r) entry of P².)
