Coins, Dice & Waiting Times

2 min read · source: quant/waiting_times.md

"Expected number of flips until X" questions are all first-step analysis: condition on the first flip, write one equation per state, solve.

Geometric distribution

Trials until first success, success probability p: E = 1/p.
Expected rolls to see a 6: six rolls. Expected flips to see heads: 2.

Analogy: if a slot machine pays out 5% of the time, you expect 20 pulls — 1/p is just "how many tries per success on average".

The famous asymmetry: HH vs HT

Expected flips to see HT: 4. To see HH: 6. Same letters, different answers.

graph LR
    S["start"] -->|"T (½)"| S
    S -->|"H (½)"| A["seen H"]
    A -->|"H (½)"| DONE["HH ✓"]
    A -->|"T (½)"| S2["back to start"]
    S2 --> S

First-step equations for HH: E₀ = 1 + ½E₀ + ½E₁ and E₁ = 1 + ½·0 + ½E₀ → E₀ = 6.

Why HT is easier: once you have an H, you can never lose progress toward HT (another H keeps you one step away; T finishes). For HH, a T after H sends you all the way back. Progress toward HT is a ratchet; toward HH it's fragile.

Penney's game (the party trick)

For any 3-flip pattern you pick, I can pick one that beats it head-to-head with probability up to 7/8 — take your first two flips, flip the first, put it in front (you pick HHT, I pick THH). Sequence-waiting is non-transitive, like rock-paper-scissors.

Dice quick hits

  • Expected number of rolls to see all six faces: coupon collector, 6·H₆ = 14.7.
  • Roll until you get a number ≥ 5: p = 1/3, E = 3 rolls.
  • Expected value of one roll: 3.5; of max of two rolls: 161/36 ≈ 4.47 (compute P(max ≤ k) = (k/6)² first — the CDF trick).

Drill

  1. Expected flips until HTH? Set up the 3-state equations. (10)
  2. A die is rolled until two consecutive rolls are equal — expected rolls? (7: after the first roll you wait for a 1/6 match.)
  3. You flip until heads; you're paid 2^(number of flips). What's E[payout] and why is the answer disturbing? (St. Petersburg — infinite.)

Seen at: Citadel, D. E. Shaw — state-equation waiting problems are the single most common quant probability genre.
