Conditional Probability & Bayes

2 min read · source: quant/toolkit.md

The only three tools you need for 80% of probability interviews: conditioning, the law of total probability, and Bayes. Everything else is bookkeeping.

Conditioning

P(A|B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B) — "of the worlds where B happened, in what fraction did A also happen?"

Analogy: conditioning is cropping the photo. B is the new frame; probabilities are re-measured inside it.

Law of Total Probability (LOTP)

Split the world into disjoint cases, weight each case:

P(A) = Σ P(A | Bᵢ) · P(Bᵢ)

If a question feels hard, the first move is almost always: condition on the first step (first flip, first card, first arrival).

Bayes — flipping the conditional

P(H | E) = P(E | H) · P(H) / P(E)

You know how likely the evidence is under each hypothesis; you want the reverse.

The worked example everyone gets wrong

A disease affects 1 in 1000 people. The test is 99% accurate both ways. You test positive — what's the chance you're sick?

graph TD
    P["1000 people"] --> S["1 sick"]
    P --> H["999 healthy"]
    S -->|"99% detected"| TP["≈ 1 true positive"]
    H -->|"1% false alarm"| FP["≈ 10 false positives"]
    TP --> ANS["positives: 1 sick vs 10 healthy<br/>P(sick | +) ≈ 1/11 ≈ 9%"]
    FP --> ANS

Not 99% — 9%. The false positives from the huge healthy pool swamp the rare true positives. This is the base-rate fallacy, and interviewers love it because the setup sounds innocent.

Independence — the fine print

  • A, B independent ⇔ P(A ∩ B) = P(A)·P(B) ⇔ P(A|B) = P(A).
  • Pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence (classic counterexample: two fair coins, events {first is H}, {second is H}, {they match}).
  • Disjoint events are never independent (unless one has probability 0) — if one happens, the other can't.

Drill

  1. Two children, "at least one is a boy" — P(both boys)? (1/3 — and see how the answer changes if you're told "the elder is a boy": 1/2.)
  2. A coin is picked from {fair, double-headed} and flipped 3 times: all heads. P(it's the double-headed one)?
  3. Re-derive the disease answer with 90% accuracy instead of 99%.

Seen at: D. E. Shaw, Citadel OAs and phone screens.
