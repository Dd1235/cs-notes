Company Prep

D. E. Shaw

2 min read · source: company_prep/deshaw.md

Quant fund whose tech interviews are an old-school rigor check: hard DSA, core CS, and an aptitude/probability layer most SWE loops don't have. Small intake, heavy filtering.

Pipeline

  1. OA — three coding problems (recent cycles: binary search, DP, prefix-sums flavour; medium→hard) plus technical MCQs, an aptitude/quant section, and sometimes an async video answer. ~300 → ~16 shortlist ratios get reported on campus.
  2. Technical round(s) (HackerRank CodePair, often two interviewers) — projects discussion, then live problems (string/substring, DP, trees) with strong follow-up pressure on complexity and edge cases.
  3. Final round — systems + CS fundamentals + behavioral; communication quality is explicitly assessed.

What they actually test

  • DSA at hard calibration — but they care how you derive, not whether you've seen it.
  • Probability & aptitude — expected values, conditional probability, puzzle logic under time (the whole quant section exists for this).
  • Core CS breadth — OS scheduling/synchronization, DBMS transactions/indexing, a layer of networking.
  • Precision of speech — they probe definitions ("what exactly is a race condition?") and punish hand-waving.

Revise on this site

Probability & Puzzles — all of it · Quant refresher · MVCC · B+ trees · Scheduling · Deadlock · Semaphore vs mutex

Practice prompts (representative, paraphrased)

  1. Expected number of rolls to see all six faces — then the interviewer changes it to "two dice per roll". Adapt live.
  2. Longest substring problems with a twist (k distinct, repeating-block structure) — narrate the sliding-window invariant.
  3. "Your query is slow" — walk index choice, isolation level, and the plan you'd expect, in DBMS terms.
  4. Timed aptitude sets (ratios, work-rate, seating logic) — speed is a separate muscle; train it separately.
