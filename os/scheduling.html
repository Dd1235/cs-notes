CPU Scheduling — FIFO to MLFQ

2 min read · source: os/scheduling.md

Scheduling policies are best learned as an evolution: each policy fixes the previous one's embarrassment. Tell it as that story in interviews.

The metrics first

  • Turnaround = completion − arrival (batch jobs care).
  • Response = first-run − arrival (interactive apps care).
  • The tension between these two is the subject.

The evolution ladder

  1. FIFO — run jobs in arrival order. Embarrassment: the convoy effect — a 3-hour job ahead of two 10-second jobs wrecks everyone's turnaround (supermarket queue behind a full cart).
  2. SJF (shortest job first) — provably optimal turnaround if jobs arrive together. Embarrassment: long job already running, short job arrives — still stuck.
  3. STCF — SJF + preemption: newcomer with less remaining time evicts the runner. Embarrassment: needs to know job lengths (nobody does), and long jobs can starve.
  4. Round Robin — time slices, rotate. Great response time; the embarrassment flips: turnaround is awful (everyone finishes late), and slice length is a knob — too short = context-switch overhead, too long = FIFO in disguise.
  5. MLFQ — the real-world answer (and OSTEP's crown jewel): multiple queues by priority; new jobs start on top; use a full slice → demote; give up CPU early (I/O) → stay high. Learns job behaviour from history — no clairvoyance needed. Patches: periodic priority boost (anti-starvation) and slice-accounting (anti-gaming).

The one-line summaries

Policy Wins Loses
FIFO simple convoy
SJF/STCF turnaround needs future knowledge, starvation
RR response turnaround
MLFQ adapts, both metrics decent tuning knobs

Real kernels

Linux CFS (now EEVDF): no fixed slices — pick the task with the least virtual runtime (weighted by nice value), from a red-black tree... conceptually "everyone should have run an equal fair share by now". One sentence is all interviews want.

Reference: OSTEP ch 7 & ch 8 — MLFQ.

Drill

  1. Jobs A=100, B=10, C=10 arrive together — average turnaround under FIFO (worst order) vs SJF?
  2. Why does MLFQ demote CPU hogs but not I/O-bound jobs, and why is that exactly what interactivity wants?
  3. How would a malicious job game naive MLFQ, and what's the fix? (Yield just before slice end; account total time at level.)
