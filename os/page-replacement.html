Page Replacement — FIFO, LRU, Clock

2 min read · source: os/page_replacement.md

Memory is full and a page fault needs a frame: who gets evicted? The policy zoo, plus the one weird anomaly they love to ask.

The setup

Demand paging brings pages in on first touch (CS:APP ch 9 has the fault path). When RAM is full, eviction policy determines the miss rate — and misses cost a disk trip, ~100,000× a RAM hit. Policy quality is not a rounding error.

The policies

  • Optimal (MIN) — evict the page used furthest in the future. Unimplementable (needs a crystal ball); exists as the benchmark.
  • FIFO — evict the oldest-loaded. Dumb: age ≠ uselessness (your shell's pages are old and hot).
  • Random — surprisingly not terrible; no state.
  • LRU — evict least-recently-used: uses history as prophecy (recent past ≈ near future = temporal locality). Problem: true LRU needs a timestamp per touch — too expensive in hardware.
  • Clock (second chance) — the shippable LRU approximation: pages in a ring, one reference bit each (set by hardware on touch). The hand sweeps: bit=1 → clear it, move on ("second chance"); bit=0 → evict. Add the dirty bit: prefer evicting clean pages (no write-back needed).

Analogy: LRU is a wardrobe rule — donate the shirt you haven't worn longest. Clock is the lazy version: sticky-note every worn shirt; sweep occasionally, unstickered shirts get donated.

Belady's anomaly (the trap question)

For FIFO, adding more frames can cause more faults (reference string 1,2,3,4,1,2,5,1,2,3,4,5: 9 faults with 3 frames, 10 with 4). LRU is immune — it's a stack algorithm: the pages held with k frames are always a subset of those with k+1. "Why is LRU immune?" — that subset property is the answer.

When LRU itself fails

Sequential scan of a huge file floods the cache with use-once pages (why databases manage their own buffer pools and Linux has separate active/inactive lists). Working set > RAM → thrashing: the system pages instead of computing; admit fewer processes or buy RAM.

Reference: OSTEP ch 22 — Swapping: Policies.

Drill

  1. Run string 7,0,1,2,0,3,0,4 through FIFO and LRU with 3 frames — count faults.
  2. Why prefer evicting clean pages? What's the cost asymmetry?
  3. Postgres scans a 100 GB table once on a 16 GB machine — what happens to a naive LRU cache, and what do real systems do? (Scan-resistant policies / ring buffers.)
