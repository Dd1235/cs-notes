Layer 2 — Switching, VLANs, STP

2 min read · source: cn/l2_switching.md

Below IP there's a whole world of MAC addresses, and it's Arista's home turf. Three mechanisms: learn, segment, don't loop.

How a switch learns (flood & learn)

A switch keeps a MAC table: MAC → port. On every incoming frame it (1) records source MAC → this port (learning), (2) forwards by destination: known → that port only; unknown or broadcast → flood everywhere except the ingress port. Entries age out (~5 min).

Analogy: a mailroom clerk who notes which desk each person's outgoing mail comes from — after a while, deliveries stop being "shout into the room" and become direct drops.

  • Hub vs switch: hub floods always (one collision domain); a switch gives each port its own collision domain.
  • A switch is transparent — hosts never know it's there. ARP (who-has-this-IP, from the URL journey) is what hosts do; MAC learning is what switches do. Interviewers love conflating them — keep them separate.

VLANs — one switch, many networks

A VLAN carves one physical switch into multiple isolated broadcast domains: port 1–8 = VLAN 10 (engineering), 9–16 = VLAN 20 (finance). Frames between switches carry an 802.1Q tag (12-bit VLAN ID) on trunk ports; access ports are untagged, single-VLAN.

Crossing VLANs requires a router (or L3 switch) — VLANs are L2 walls; IP is the door. Benefits: blast-radius control for broadcasts, security segmentation, no rewiring to move a user.

Spanning Tree — don't loop

Two switches cabled twice (for redundancy) = a loop, and Ethernet frames have no TTL: one broadcast circulates forever, amplifying — a broadcast storm that melts the network in seconds.

STP (802.1D) fixes it: switches elect a root (lowest bridge ID), compute shortest paths to it, and block every redundant port — the topology becomes a tree (spanning tree, literally). Link dies → blocked port unblocks (~seconds; RSTP makes it fast). The price of loop-freedom: blocked links carry nothing — modern datacenters often prefer L3 designs partly to reclaim that capacity.

Drill

  1. Host A pings host B through a fresh switch — narrate every flood, ARP, and MAC-table entry from silence to steady state.
  2. Two PCs on different VLANs, same switch, same IP subnet — can they talk? (No — no L2 path, and no router will route within one subnet.)
  3. Why does Ethernet need STP when IP survives loops fine? (IP has TTL; frames don't.)

Prime Arista territory — they build these boxes.
