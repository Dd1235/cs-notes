TLS — Handshake & Certificates

2 min read · source: cn/tls.md

Two problems, two tools: agree on a secret with a stranger (asymmetric crypto, once) then talk fast (symmetric crypto, everything after). TLS is the choreography between them.

Why two kinds of crypto

  • Symmetric (AES): same key both sides — fast (hardware instructions), but how do you share the key over a tapped wire?
  • Asymmetric (RSA/elliptic-curve): public/private pairs — solves key sharing, but ~1000× slower.
  • So: use asymmetric once to establish a shared symmetric key, then AES for the actual data. Every TLS connection is this pattern.

TLS 1.3 handshake (one round trip)

  1. ClientHello: supported ciphers + client's Diffie-Hellman public share (+ ALPN: "I speak h2/h3" — from the URL journey).
  2. ServerHello: server's DH share → both sides now compute the same shared secret (DH: mix public paints with private paint; eavesdroppers see only the mixed publics and can't unmix). Server also sends its certificate and proves it holds the private key by signing the transcript.
  3. Client verifies the certificate chain, both derive symmetric session keys — encrypted app data flows on the next packet.

TLS 1.2 needed two round trips and supported non-forward-secret RSA key exchange; 1.3 removed all that (and 0-RTT resumption exists for repeat visitors, with replay caveats). "Handshake = 1-RTT in 1.3" is the number to know.

Certificates — why you trust a stranger

DH stops eavesdroppers but not a man-in-the-middle doing DH with each side separately. Certificates fix identity: the server's public key is signed by a CA, whose key is signed up a chain ending at a root CA shipped inside your OS/browser.

Verification = walk the chain: signature valid → name matches (SAN covers *.google.com) → dates valid → not revoked. Any failure = the browser warning screen.

Analogy: a certificate is a passport: you trust it not because you know the traveler, but because you recognize the issuing government's seal — and forging the seal is the hard part.

Interview one-liners

  • "What does HTTPS actually give you?" — confidentiality (encryption), integrity (AEAD/MACs), authentication of the server (certs). Client auth is optional/rare (mTLS in service meshes).
  • Forward secrecy: keys are ephemeral per-connection — stealing the server's long-term key later can't decrypt recorded traffic. This is why 1.3 made DH mandatory.
  • Where's the overhead? One extra RTT at setup + cheap AES after. "TLS is slow" stopped being true a decade ago.

Drill

  1. Draw the 1.3 handshake and mark the exact packet where encryption begins.
  2. Corporate proxies decrypt employee HTTPS — what must they install on laptops and why does it work? (A private root CA into the trust store — you've re-rooted trust.)
  3. Why does forward secrecy require ephemeral DH rather than RSA key transport?

Relevant to Stripe (API infrastructure) and Arista.
