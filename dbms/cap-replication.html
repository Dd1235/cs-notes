CAP & Replication

2 min read · source: dbms/cap_replication.md

The ACID notes ended with "read-after-update breaks once you have two servers". This page is why, and what everyone does about it.

Replication — why and how

Copies of the data on multiple machines buy you: surviving a dead server (availability), reads served near users (latency), and read scale-out. The standard shape is leader/follower: writes go to the leader, the leader ships its change log (the WAL, again) to followers, reads can go anywhere.

  • Synchronous replication — leader confirms commit only after follower(s) have it. No data loss on failover; every write pays the network round trip, and a dead follower can block writes.
  • Asynchronous — leader confirms immediately, ships later. Fast; a leader crash loses the unshipped tail.
  • Real systems: semi-sync compromises (one sync follower), or quorums (write to W of N, read from R of N; W+R>N ⇒ reads see latest — the Dynamo/Cassandra formulation).

Replication lag — the bug you'll actually meet

Async follower is seconds behind → user updates profile (leader), refreshes (follower) → sees the old profile. Fixes worth naming: read-your-writes (pin the user to the leader briefly), monotonic reads (same replica per session), or just accept staleness where it's harmless (like counts).

CAP, in plain words

When a network partition happens (and it will), a distributed store must pick:

  • CP — refuse some requests to stay consistent (a minority-side bank node returns errors rather than stale balances).
  • AP — keep answering everywhere, reconcile later (shopping carts merge; DNS serves stale records happily).

Two corrections that signal you actually understand it: P is not optional — the choice only exists during a partition (normal operation trades latency vs consistency instead — PACELC name-drop); and it's per-operation, not a database's zodiac sign (a "CP" system can serve stale reads by config, e.g. quorum settings).

Analogy: two branch managers lose their phone line. CP: "no big withdrawals till the line's back." AP: "approve everything, reconcile the ledgers tonight" — and maybe two people withdraw the same money.

Where familiar systems sit (defaults, roughly)

Single-node Postgres: CAP doesn't apply — that's the luxury of one box. Postgres + async replicas: CP-ish writes, stale reads. Cassandra/Dynamo: tunable via R/W quorums. Zookeeper/etcd: CP by design (that's their job).

Drill

  1. Payment service: which replication mode, and what exactly is lost if the leader dies in async mode? (The acked-but-unshipped tail — for payments, unacceptable → sync/semi-sync.)
  2. Design read-your-writes for a profile service on async replicas — two mechanisms.
  3. Why does W+R>N guarantee overlap? Draw N=3, W=2, R=2.

System-design rounds at Stripe and Rubrik lean on exactly this.
